# Research

## Summary

This page serves as a central entry point for the author's speculative and exploratory research program. It is not a record of a single narrow specialization. Instead, it outlines a broader effort to develop connected lines of inquiry across [[Mathematics]] and [[Computer science]], with particular emphasis on mathematics as the primary and organizing domain.

The work collected under this heading includes formal questions, conceptual experiments, unfinished frameworks, and long-range ambitions. Some parts are close to established mathematical or computational traditions. Others are openly speculative. For that reason, this section of the wiki should not be read as a list of settled results. It should be read as a map of active directions, recurring problems, and research motives.

Its purpose is to gather these directions into one place so that they can be clarified, expanded, linked, and revised over time.

## Purpose of this section

The purpose of the research section is to provide a stable structure for ongoing intellectual work.

Many speculative ideas are lost because they remain scattered across notebooks, chats, code comments, isolated reflections, and unfinished drafts. Once separated from one another, they become difficult to compare, refine, or develop. This section exists to prevent that dispersion.

It is intended to do several things at once:

- preserve speculative research directions in written form
    
- distinguish developed ideas from preliminary intuitions
    
- connect technical work to broader conceptual goals
    
- organize questions by field, subfield, and long-term ambition
    
- create a reusable internal framework for future articles
    

This section is therefore less like a finished academic publication and more like a structured research habitat.

## General character of the research

The research collected here is marked by several recurring features.

First, it is **structural**. It is usually less interested in isolated facts than in systems, transformations, expressive frameworks, and deep correspondences between domains.

Second, it is **integrative**. It often seeks bridges between traditions that are normally treated separately, such as analysis and logic, symbolic systems and continuous systems, formal languages and geometric or operator-theoretic frameworks.

Third, it is **formal but exploratory**. The work values rigor, but it also makes room for high-level speculative intuition at early stages.

Fourth, it is **generative** rather than merely interpretive. The aim is not only to summarize existing knowledge, but to formulate new ways of representing or organizing it.

Fifth, it is **personal in emphasis**. The project does not attempt to imitate the priorities of a standard university department. It follows recurring conceptual obsessions and long-range questions.

## Why mathematics is central

Mathematics is the main center of gravity for this research program because it provides the deepest available language for exact structure, abstraction, and transformation.

The mathematical side of the project is not confined to conventional problem-solving within an existing subfield. It includes broader ambitions such as:

- finding deep correspondences between mathematical traditions
    
- treating the continuum as a possible universal representational medium
    
- studying symbolic systems through analytic or algebraic encoding
    
- developing new structural viewpoints that cut across established categories
    
- building formal tools that can support wider theoretical unification
    

In this sense, mathematics is not only one research area among others. It is the primary medium through which the rest of the intellectual project is organized.

## Why computer science is included

[[Computer science]] appears here not as a separate practical trade detached from theory, but as a neighboring formal domain with strong two-way interaction with mathematics.

Computer science is relevant to this research program for several reasons:

- it provides explicit models of computation and representation
    
- it studies formal systems, semantics, and symbolic transformation
    
- it offers executable frameworks for testing abstract ideas
    
- it turns theoretical structures into concrete software artifacts
    
- it raises foundational questions about expression, complexity, and implementation
    

For this reason, computer science functions both as an object of study and as an instrument for extending mathematical thought into executable systems.

## Kinds of material that belong here

The pages in this section may include several kinds of content.

### Research overviews

These summarize a whole line of inquiry and explain what motivates it, what problems it addresses, and what its current status is.

### Conceptual sketches

These are early attempts to formulate an idea before it reaches a full formal shape.

### Formal ambitions

These describe desired theories, conjectural frameworks, or hoped-for unifications that may still be far from completion.

### Methodological notes

These concern how to investigate a problem, what tools might apply, or how one field may be translated into another.

### Research questions

These state precise or semi-precise questions that can guide later formal work.

### Programmatic statements

These describe the long-term direction of an entire research thread rather than a single theorem or construction.

## Relation to established mathematics and computer science

This section includes both expository and speculative material. That distinction matters.

Some pages will summarize established concepts in a careful and conventional way. Others will develop personal research aims that are not standard and may not yet be formally justified. This wiki should preserve that distinction clearly.

A useful working separation is:

- **Expository pages** explain accepted or standard material.
    
- **Research pages** describe active lines of inquiry, tentative frameworks, open ambitions, and possible future theories.
    

This separation is important because it lets the wiki support both learning and invention without confusing the two.

## Long-term function of this section

In the long run, this research section is meant to become a navigable map of the author's own theoretical world.

That includes:

- major recurring themes
    
- long-range formal ambitions
    
- unresolved questions
    
- bridges between fields
    
- candidate foundational ideas
    
- partially developed frameworks
    
- supporting technical concepts
    

Over time, the goal is that these pages will allow speculative thought to become progressively more explicit, more connected, and more testable.
